Air Quality and Obesity in the U.S.: A Decade of Diverging Trends

Over the past decade, two major public health concerns—air pollution and obesity—have shown persistent and troubling trends across the United States. While environmental regulations have led to measurable improvements in air quality, obesity rates have continued to rise, particularly in the South and Midwest. This report explores the geographic and temporal patterns of these two issues, focusing on New York as a case study, and investigates whether there is a meaningful relationship between air pollution (PM2.5 levels) and physical activity or obesity rates.

Through visualizations and data analysis, we examine whether cleaner air correlates with healthier behaviors and outcomes, and identify regions where both pollution and obesity are prevalent. Our findings suggest that while air quality improvements are real, they do not automatically translate into reduced obesity or increased physical activity—highlighting the need for integrated public health strategies.
