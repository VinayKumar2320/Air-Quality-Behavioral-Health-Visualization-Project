Air Quality and Obesity in the U.S.: A Decade of Trends

Over the past decade, two major public health concerns—air pollution and obesity—have demonstrated persistent and diverging patterns across the United States. While stricter environmental regulations have driven measurable improvements in air quality, obesity rates continue to rise, particularly across the South and Midwest.

This study visualizes geographic and temporal patterns in both air pollution and health metrics, with a focus on New York as a case study. We explore whether cleaner air correlates with increased physical activity or reduced obesity rates, and identify regions experiencing both high pollution and high obesity levels.

Data Description

Sources: EPA Air Quality System, CDC BRFSS, NYC Open Data, U.S. Census Bureau

Attributes: Year (2011–2023), PM2.5 Levels, Obesity Rate, Physical Activity Rate, Region/State

Research Questions & Findings

1. Geographic Patterns of Obesity and Activity

Southern and Midwestern states consistently demonstrate the highest obesity rates—exceeding 35% by 2022. Meanwhile, Western and Northeastern states show comparatively healthier trends. These disparities often reflect systemic differences in built environment, socioeconomic conditions, and public health investment.

2. Air Quality Trends Over Time

Between 2011 and 2016, national PM2.5 levels declined significantly due to emissions regulations. Progress plateaued in later years, driven partly by increasing wildfire events. New York City improved from ~9 µg/m³ to ~7 µg/m³ across this period.

3. Correlation Between Air Quality and Physical Activity

Certain areas, such as Flushing and Whitestone (CD7), show a strong negative correlation between PM2.5 levels and physical activity, indicating that pollution spikes may discourage outdoor exercise.

4. Temporal Overlap of Air Quality and Obesity

Improvements in air quality do not appear to directly translate into reductions in obesity. Pollution affects short-term behavior (activity levels) more than long-term body weight outcomes.

5. Dual-Burden Regions

States like Louisiana and Mississippi face both high pollution and high obesity burdens, making them ideal targets for integrated environmental and public health interventions.

Conclusion

Air quality has improved over the last decade, but obesity continues to rise. While pollution reduces physical activity in the short term, obesity—a long-term, multifactorial condition—does not respond immediately. The findings emphasize the need for policies that simultaneously address environmental factors and lifestyle/behavioral health.
